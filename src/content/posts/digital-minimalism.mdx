Most digital minimalism advice is a detox. Delete the apps, go grayscale for a week, feel virtuous, reinstall everything by Friday. That never held for me. What holds is treating it as a setup, not a sprint: make the distracting thing harder to reach than the useful thing, once, and then stop relying on willpower.

Two layers do most of the work.

## The phone is deliberately limited

The phone is the part people notice. I keep it boring on purpose. The point is not to punish myself, it is to remove the slot-machine surfaces so the phone goes back to being a tool I pick up for a reason and put down when the reason is done.

## The computer is where the work and the distractions share a screen

The phone is easy mode. The hard case is the computer, because the same machine holds the deep work and the best distractions. So the blocking lives there too, on the surfaces that pull hardest during a focus block.

## Why bother, when nobody would notice

This is the part that ties back to everything else I am working on. None of these edges is decisive on its own. A slightly less distracting phone. A blocked feed. Knowing your numbers cold. Standing up straight. Being the one who did the background reading. Each is a rounding error. Stacked, they are the difference, and most of them are quietly available to anyone willing to set them up once.

A boring phone is one of the cheapest one-percent edges I have found. The setup cost is an afternoon. The return compounds every day after.
